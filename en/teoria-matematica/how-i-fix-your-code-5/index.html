How I Fix Your Code - 5

Finally we made it to this last part. It has been a very long journey, but we got to the end. We started from simple concepts like parity, then reached linear codes and went through cyclic ones, with pit stops to talk about Hamming and Reed-Solomon. One last piece is missing: understanding how error correction works in Reed-Solomon codes. For Hamming it was fairly simple; here it is much less intuitive, and by the end of this article you will understand why I chose to split it into three. It is time to talk about Berlekamp-Massey, let's begin.

Duke Berlekamp and Count Massey

Let's start by saying that there are several algorithms to correct errors in Reed-Solomon codes, but the one we will see is the Berlekamp-Massey method, called BMA. We will not go too much into the details of the algorithm; we will stick to our goal, namely correcting the error. We already saw how to compute the syndrome, and we know that it is nothing more than a vector:

\[ \vec{s} = (S_1, \, S_2, \, \cdots, \, S_{d-1}) \]

BMA uses the individual components of \(\vec{s}\) starting from the idea that they are not independent from each other, but reflect the structure of the error (or errors) present in \(\vec{c}\). Let's see why. From the correction condition, we know that \(t\) is the maximum number of errors we can correct. Now suppose we have a wrong codeword. This can be written as the sum of a correct codeword and an error vector:

\[ \vec{c}_e = \vec{c} + \vec{e} \]

and the error vector is a vector that has \(0\) everywhere except in \(\nu\) positions, with \(\nu \leq t\). Each error component is characterized by two pieces of information:

  1. Where the error is, that is, in which position of the codeword
  2. How much the error is, that is, the non-zero value that appears in that position

This means that, in the worst case where there are exactly \(t\) errors, we must determine \(t\) positions and \(t\) values, for a total of \(2t\) unknowns. At this point the syndrome components come into play. As we have seen, the syndrome is completely determined by the received codeword and therefore by the error vector. In particular, each component \(S_i\) depends on all the elements of \(\vec{e}\) (if this step is not clear, read here), and therefore \(\vec{s}\) contains information about the errors, and its components depend on the errors themselves. To fully determine the errors we need a sufficient amount of this information. Since, as any good engineer, you should always consider the worst case, and since the unknowns are \(2t\), we will need at least \(2t\) syndrome components. Therefore BMA uses the first \(2t\) components of \(\vec{s}\), that is

\[ S_1, \, S_2, \, \cdots, \, S_{2t} \]

It can be shown that the syndrome components satisfy a linear relation of the form

\[ S_i + \Lambda_1 \cdot S_{i-1} + \Lambda_2 \cdot S_{i-2} + \cdots + \Lambda_{\nu} \cdot S_{i-\nu} = 0 \]

Formula 1. Relation Between Syndrome Components

where:

  • \(\nu\) represents the order of the relation and depends on the number of errors present; in fact \(0 \leq \nu \leq t\)
  • \(i\) is the index of the syndrome component considered and must satisfy the condition \(i \geq \nu + 1\)

To better understand, if we have one error then \(\nu = 1\) and \(i = 2\) (but \(i = 3, 4, \dots\) would also work), so we have

\[ S_2 + \Lambda_1 \cdot S_1 = 0 \]

With two errors we would have \(\nu = 2\) and \(i = 3\), hence

\[ \begin{aligned} S_3 + \Lambda_1 \cdot S_2 + \Lambda_2 \cdot S_1 = 0 \\ S_4 + \Lambda_1 \cdot S_3 + \Lambda_2 \cdot S_2 = 0 \\ \end{aligned} \]

Back to the general case. From the previous relation, what we want to do is determine the coefficients

\[ \Lambda_1, \, \Lambda_2, \, \cdots, \, \Lambda_{\nu} \]

which are our unknowns. For convenience, these coefficients are collected into a single object by introducing the polynomial

\[ \Lambda(X) = 1 + \Lambda_1 \cdot X + \Lambda_2 \cdot X^2 + \cdots + \Lambda_{\nu} \cdot X^{\nu} \]

Formula 2. Error Locator Polynomial

called the error locator polynomial. The problem is that we do not know in advance either the degree \(\nu\) or the coefficients of the polynomial, because we do not know how many errors occurred. For this reason, BMA builds \(\Lambda(X)\) iteratively: we start from the simplest hypothesis and, using the syndrome components one at a time, we check whether the current polynomial is compatible with the data. If this compatibility does not hold at the next steps, the degree of the polynomial is increased. To perform this check, we evaluate the previous relation by introducing a quantity called discrepancy:

\[ \Delta_i = S_i + \Lambda_1 \cdot S_{i-1} + \Lambda_2 \cdot S_{i-2} + \cdots + \Lambda_{\nu} \cdot S_{i-\nu} \]

Formula 3. Discrepancy

The discrepancy is the value of the relation between the syndrome components at step \(i\) and it is studied at two levels: local and global. At the local level it allows us to determine the coefficients of the error locator polynomial, and it can be summarized like this:

  • If \(\Delta_i = 0\): the current polynomial is compatible with the syndrome component \(S_i\).
  • If \(\Delta_i \neq 0\): the current polynomial does not correctly describe the syndrome components considered up to that point, and therefore it must be updated.

At the global level, instead, we check whether the choice made is compatible also with the next syndrome components. For example, we can use \(\Delta_i\) to determine the coefficients \(\Lambda_k\) of the polynomial and \(\Delta_{i+1}\) to verify whether the obtained polynomial is still compatible:

  • If \(\Delta_{i+1} = 0\): the choice is consistent and the locator polynomial is compatible with the syndrome components up to that point.
  • If \(\Delta_{i+1} \neq 0\): the assumed degree is not sufficient and the polynomial must be updated to one of higher degree.

(don't worry, with the examples it will be clearer). In this way, BMA progressively builds the locator polynomial, fixing it every time the discrepancy turns out non-zero, until it obtains a polynomial compatible with the first \(2t\) syndrome components and of minimum degree, sufficient to describe the errors present. If additional syndrome components are available, it is possible to verify compatibility at later steps as well, reinforcing the validity of the chosen degree.

Once we have the polynomial, we solve it by finding its roots, thus setting \(\Lambda(X)=0\). If the polynomial has degree \(1\) it is fairly easy to solve, but if the degree is higher things get complicated. Since its roots belong to the finite field on which the code is built, we could just apply brute force to find them all, but if the field is very large then obviously this is not feasible. Luckily, there is an efficient algorithm to find the roots of a polynomial over a finite field, called the Chien algorithm, which, don't worry, I will not explain.

You're welcome.

In any case, it can be shown that its roots are directly linked to the error positions in the codeword. In particular, in Reed-Solomon codes this link is given by the definition of the parity-check matrix. It was in fact defined via powers of the primitive root

\[ H_{i,j} = \alpha^{i \cdot j} \]

Therefore, to each position \(j\) of the codeword we associate the element \(\alpha^j\) of the field. The locator polynomial is built so as to cancel these powers, so its roots have the form

\[ X = \alpha^{-j} \]

So by solving

\[ \Lambda(X) = 0 \]

Formula 4. Solving Condition

we obtain the \(\alpha^{-j}\) from which it is possible to recover the position \(j\) of the error in the codeword.

Once the error positions are identified, we still need to determine their value. This can also be done using the syndrome components, which contain all the necessary information about the errors. In particular, knowing the error positions, it is possible to build a system of equations using the syndrome components. In fact, each component can be expressed as

\[ S_i = \sum_{k=1}^{\nu} e_k \cdot \alpha^{i \cdot j_k} \]

Formula 5. Relation Between Syndrome And Error

Where \(j_k\) represents the position of the \(k\)-th error, \(e_k\) its value, and \(i\) is the \(i\)-th element of the syndrome \(\vec{s}\). This formula allows us to obtain a system of equations from which we can determine the error values \(e_k\) and at that point the correction is immediate, we just need to do the subtraction:

\[ \vec{c} = \vec{c}_e - \vec{e} = (c_{e_{1}}, \, c_{e_{2}}, \, \cdots, \, c_{e_{n}}) - (e_1, \, e_2, \, \cdots, \, e_n) \]

to obtain the original codeword.

Ok, it sounds complicated like this, but as usual let's see it with some examples.

Ars Goetia 1

Let's go back to the example we started here. The syndrome for the corrupted codeword was:

\[ \vec{s} = (S_1, S_2, S_3) = (5, 4, 6)^T \]

From the theory we know that we must take the first \(2t\) elements of the syndrome, and since the code defined in the example can correct at most \(t=1\) errors, we take the first two elements of the syndrome:

\[ S_1 = 5, \quad S_2 = 4 \]

The error locator polynomial has the form:

\[ \Lambda(X) = 1 + \Lambda_1 \cdot X \]

and we must determine the coefficient \(\Lambda_1\). Once the polynomial is known, we can then determine its roots. We have one equation in two unknowns, but no worries, there is always the discrepancy we can use. As said, its computation is iterative and since the condition holds:

\[ i \geq \nu + 1 \implies i \geq 2 \]

we start from \(i=2\):

\[ \Delta_2 = S_2 + \Lambda_1 \cdot S_1 = 4 + \Lambda_1 \cdot 5 \]

We impose \(\Delta_2 = 0\) and solve for \(\Lambda_1\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \Lambda_1 \cdot 5 &= -4 \pmod{7} \\ \Lambda_1 \cdot 5 &= 3 \pmod{7} \\ \Lambda_1 &= 5^{-1} \cdot 3 \pmod{7} \\ \Lambda_1 &= 3 \cdot 3 \pmod{7} \\ \Lambda_1 &= 2 \pmod{7} \end{aligned} \]

(if you don't know why these steps work, read here). By imposing \(\Delta_2 = 0\) we obtain the coefficient \(\Lambda_1\). We then check whether this choice also cancels the next discrepancy. Let's do it:

\[ \begin{aligned} \Delta_3 &= S_3 + \Lambda_1 S_2 \\ &= 6 + 2 \cdot 4 \pmod{7} \\ &= 6 + 8 \\ &= 14 \equiv 0 \pmod{7} \end{aligned} \]

Since the \(\Lambda_1\) found from \(\Delta_2\) also cancels \(\Delta_3\), we can conclude that the degree \(1\) locator polynomial is sufficient (and thank goodness, since the code defined could correct at most \(t=1\) error, so we could not go any further anyway).

\[ \Lambda(X) = 1 + \Lambda_1 \cdot X \]

and by setting \(\Lambda(X) = 0 \pmod{7}\) we obtain:

\[ \begin{aligned} 1 + \Lambda_1 \cdot X &= 0 \\ 1 + 2 \cdot X &= 0 \\ 2 \cdot X &= -1 \pmod{7} \\ 2 \cdot X &= 6 \pmod{7} \\ X &= 2^{-1} \cdot 6 \pmod{7} \\ X &= 4 \cdot 6 \pmod{7} \\ X &= 3 \pmod{7} \end{aligned} \]

Since \(X = \alpha^{-j}\), and since the primitive root is \(\alpha = 3\), we have that

\[ \begin{aligned} 3 &= 3^{-j} \pmod{7} \\ 3 \cdot 3^j &= 3^{-j} \cdot 3^j \pmod{7} \\ 3^{j+1} &= 1 \pmod{7} \\ \end{aligned} \]

By the cyclicity of powers in the multiplicative group, we have that \(3^{j+1} = 1 \pmod{7}\) is true if:

\[ j + 1 = 0 \pmod{6} \Rightarrow j = 5 \]

and therefore the error is in position \(j = 5\) of the codeword. Now go check the example and tell me if this is cool or not. Alright, I'll write it here: the wrong codeword was

\[ \vec{c}_e = (6, \, 6, \, 2, \, 3, \, 4, \, \textcolor{red}{4}) \]

where the number in red is the error, and in which position is it?!

At this point, by applying the formula we can derive the value of the error itself:

\[ \begin{aligned} S_1 &= e_1 \cdot \alpha^{1 \cdot 5} \\ 5 &= e_1 \cdot 3^5 \pmod{7} \\ 5 &= e_1 \cdot 5 \pmod{7} \\ e_1 &= 5^{-1} \cdot 5 \pmod{7} \\ e_1 &= 1 \pmod{7} \end{aligned} \]

and therefore the error vector is

\[ \vec{e} = (0, \, 0, \, 0, \, 0, \, 0, \, 1) \]

and subtracting this from the wrong codeword we obtain the correct codeword:

\[ \begin{aligned} \vec{c} &= \vec{c}_e - \vec{e} \\ \vec{c} &= (6, \, 6, \, 2, \, 3, \, 4, \, \textcolor{red}{4}) - (0, \, 0, \, 0, \, 0, \, 0, \, 1) \\ \vec{c} &= (6, \, 6, \, 2, \, 3, \, 4, \, 3) \end{aligned} \]

and go here to see what the correct codeword was. If this isn't cool, then I don't know what is.

Note that I used

\[ S_1 = e_1 \cdot \alpha^{1 \cdot 5} \]

but any syndrome component would have worked. For example, choosing \(i=2\) we have:

\[ \begin{aligned} S_2 &= e_1 \cdot \alpha^{2 \cdot 5} \\ 4 &= e_1 \cdot 3^{10} \pmod{7} \\ 4 &= e_1 \cdot 3^{4} \pmod{7} \\ 4 &= e_1 \cdot 4 \pmod{7} \\ e_1 &= 1 \pmod{7} \end{aligned} \]

and again with \(i=3\):

\[ \begin{aligned} S_3 &= e_1 \cdot \alpha^{3 \cdot 5} \\ 6 &= e_1 \cdot 3^{15} \pmod{7} \\ 6 &= e_1 \cdot 3^{3} \pmod{7} \\ 6 &= e_1 \cdot 6 \pmod{7} \\ e_1 &= 1 \pmod{7} \end{aligned} \]

Ars Goetia 2

This will not be a complete example like the previous one, but it is more for didactic purposes. In fact, the case with one error is fairly simple, but if there are two errors things get a bit more complicated. Suppose we have the following syndrome:

\[ \vec{s} = (S_1, S_2, S_3, S_4) = (5, 6, 0, 6)^T \]

and suppose that the code (built over \(\mathbb{F}_7\) and with \(\alpha=3\)) considered is able to correct up to \(t=2\) errors, so we take the first \(2t = 4\) syndrome elements. Since we are not Nostradamus, we cannot know whether \(\nu=1\) or \(\nu=2\) errors occurred, so we start by assuming that \(1\) occurred. Therefore we compute the discrepancy at step \(i=2\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \Delta_2 &= S_2 + \Lambda_1 \cdot S_1 \\ &= 6 + \Lambda_1 \cdot 5 \pmod{7} \\ 6 + 5\Lambda_1 &= 0 \pmod{7} \\ 5\Lambda_1 &= -6 \equiv 1 \pmod{7} \\ \Lambda_1 &= 5^{-1} \cdot 1 \pmod{7} \\ &= 3 \end{aligned} \]

Canceling \(\Delta_2\) only tells us that a degree \(1\) polynomial is compatible with the syndrome up to that step. To understand whether this hypothesis is actually sufficient, we must also check the next step, that is compute \(\Delta_3\).

\[ \begin{aligned} \Delta_3 &= S_3 + \Lambda_1 \cdot S_2 \\ &= 0 + 3 \cdot 6 \pmod{7} \\ &= 18 \equiv 4 \pmod{7} \end{aligned} \]

So \(\Delta_3 \neq 0\), and this shows that the hypothesis \(\nu = 1\) is not sufficient to describe the syndrome. Therefore we must increase the degree of the locator polynomial and move to the case \(\nu = 2\), that is consider a polynomial of the form

\[ \Lambda(X) = 1 + \Lambda_1 \cdot X + \Lambda_2 \cdot X^2 \]

In this case, the discrepancy formula is

\[ \Delta_i = S_i + \Lambda_1 \cdot S_{i-1} + \Lambda_2 \cdot S_{i-2} \]

from the theory we know that

\[ i \geq \nu + 1 \implies i \geq 3 \]

Note that this does not contradict the fact that before we started from \(i=2\), because we were still testing the hypothesis \(\nu = 1\), while now we moved to the case \(\nu = 2\). But back to us: we therefore consider steps \(i=3\) and \(i=4\). In this way we obtain two equations in the two unknowns \(\Lambda_1\) and \(\Lambda_2\), imposing again that the discrepancy is zero:

\[ \begin{cases} \Delta_3 = 0 \\ \Delta_4 = 0 \end{cases} \Rightarrow \begin{cases} 0 + 6 \cdot \Lambda_1 + 5 \cdot \Lambda_2 = 0 \\ 6 + 0 \cdot \Lambda_1 + 6 \cdot \Lambda_2 = 0 \end{cases} \]

from which

\[ \Lambda_1 = 2, \quad \Lambda_2 = 6 \]

At this point it makes no sense to continue: we already used all the available syndrome components (\(2t\)) and obtained a degree \(2\) polynomial that cancels the discrepancies. This is the maximum degree compatible with the number of correctable errors, so the locator polynomial is determined and it is

\[ \Lambda(X) = 1 + 2 \cdot X + 6 \cdot X^2 \]

and we must solve

\[ \Lambda(X) = 0 \pmod{7} \]

We will not do the calculations in full, so trust me when I say that the solutions are

\[ X_1 = 4, \quad X_2 = 5 \]

Remembering that the roots have the form \(X = \alpha^{-j}\), we write

\[ \begin{aligned} 3^{-j_1} &= 4 \pmod{7} \\ 3^{-j_2} &= 5 \pmod{7} \end{aligned} \]

from which, using the cyclicity of the multiplicative group,

\[ j_1 = 2, \quad j_2 = 1 \]

and therefore we know that the errors are in positions \(1\) and \(2\). Finally, by applying this formula and using \(S_1 = 5\) and \(S_2 = 6\) (therefore \(i=1\) and \(i=2\)) we can derive the error values:

\[ \begin{cases} 5 = e_1 \cdot 3^{1 \cdot 2} + e_2 \cdot 3^{1 \cdot 1} \pmod{7} \\ 6 = e_1 \cdot 3^{2 \cdot 2} + e_2 \cdot 3^{2 \cdot 1} \pmod{7} \end{cases} \Rightarrow \begin{cases} 5 = 2 \cdot e_1 + 3 \cdot e_2 \pmod{7} \\ 6 = 4 \cdot e_1 + 2 \cdot e_2 \pmod{7} \end{cases} \]

from which we derive \(e_1\) and \(e_2\) and then build the error vector by associating each value \(e_k\) to the corresponding position \(j_k\). In this case, since we found \(j_1 = 2\) and \(j_2 = 1\), we obtain:

\[ \vec{e} = (0, \, e_2, \, e_1, \, 0, \, 0, \, 0) \]

(obviously index counting starts from \(0\)) and to conclude

\[ \vec{c} = \vec{c}_e - \vec{e} \]

If, after applying the correction, recomputing the syndrome we obtain

\[ \vec{s}=0 \]

it means that the resulting word is compatible with the code (that is, it is a codeword).
