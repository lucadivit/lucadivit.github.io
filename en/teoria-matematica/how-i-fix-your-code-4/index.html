In the previous article, we focused on talking about cyclic codes. In this one we will look at the cyclic code par excellence, the Reed-Solomon code. Given it has been a while that I have been saying I want to talk about it, and if my calculations are correct this article will be published in June 2026, I would say it is time to keep my word. So let's begin...

Clavicula Salomonis

We need to introduce the concept of a primitive root. Let's start with the simple case and consider a finite field \(\mathbb{F}_p\) with \(p\) prime. A primitive root is an element \(\alpha \in \mathbb{F}_p\) such that:

  1. Its powers for \(x \in \{1, \dots, p - 1 \}\) generate all the elements of \(\mathbb{F}_p\) except \(0\).
  2. The power to \(p-1\) generates \(1\).

Yes, as usual said like this it sounds like some terribly complicated thing, but let's see it with an example. Consider \(p=7\), then

\[ \mathbb{F}_7 = \{ 0, \, 1, \, 2, \, 3, \, 4, \, 5, \, 6 \} \]

is a field. If you do not know why, read my article on finite fields. Given the field, take the element \(\alpha = 3\) and start testing all the powers:

\[ \begin{aligned} 3^1 &\equiv 3 \pmod{7} \quad & 3^2 &\equiv 2 \pmod{7} \quad & 3^3 &\equiv 6 \pmod{7} \\ 3^4 &\equiv 4 \pmod{7} \quad & 3^5 &\equiv 5 \pmod{7} \quad & 3^6 &\equiv 1 \pmod{7} \end{aligned} \]

As you can see, the powers of \(\alpha\) generate all the elements belonging to the field and for \(p-1=6\) the result is \(1\). So \(\alpha = 3\) is a primitive root of \(\mathbb{F}_7\), and we can say it is a generator of the multiplicative group.

Good, let's continue. Suppose we have a finite field with \(q=p^m\) elements where \(p\) is a prime number, take a primitive root \(\alpha\) and choose a value \(d\) (which manages the redundancy) such that

\[ 1 \leq d < n \]

where \(n = q - 1\). Then the generator polynomial of the Reed-Solomon code, which is built over a Galois field and not over a ring, is given by

\[ \begin{aligned} g(X) &= \prod_{i=1}^{d - 1} \left( X - \alpha^i \right) \\ &= \left( X - \alpha \right) \cdot \left( X - \alpha^2 \right) \dots \left( X - \alpha^{d - 1} \right) \pmod{q} \end{aligned} \]

Formula 1. Reed-Solomon Generator Polynomial

Expanding this polynomial is not particularly difficult because the powers of \(\alpha\) can be reduced as shown here. As seen here, starting from \(g(X)\) and considering the product

\[ g(X) \cdot f(X) \]

we obtain the set of polynomials that form the generator matrix \(G\). In the Reed-Solomon case, moreover, \(f(X)\) has maximum degree \(n - d\) so the number of coefficients (that is, the number of message symbols) is

\[ k = n - d + 1 \]

and \(n\) is the length of the codeword. Note that here we talk about symbols and not bits, since we are working over a field. Having defined \(k\) and \(n\), we can write that the generator matrix \(G\) has size \(k \times n\), where for each row we put the coefficients of \(g(X)\), padding and shifting exactly as done here. Since in the field \(\mathbb{F}_q\) we have \(q\) elements and the number of coefficients in \(f(X)\) is \(k\), we have that the number of codewords in \(C\) is \(q^k\). Moreover, it can be shown that the Reed-Solomon code is MDS and maximizes the error correction capability for the same codeword length.

We have defined the generator matrix, now we define the parity-check matrix \(H\). Starting from the condition that

\[ G \cdot H^T = 0 \]

it can be shown (but we will not do it) that \(H\) can be constructed as follows:

\[ H_{i,j} = \alpha^{i \cdot j}, \quad i = 1,\dots,d-1,\; \text{ e } \; j = 0,\dots,n-1 \]

with \(i\) as the row index and \(j\) as the column index. \(H\) therefore has size \((d - 1) \times n\) and by expanding it we have:

\[ H = \begin{bmatrix} \alpha^{1 \cdot 0} & \alpha^{1 \cdot 1} & \alpha^{1 \cdot 2} & \cdots & \alpha^{1 \cdot (n-1)} \\ \alpha^{2 \cdot 0} & \alpha^{2 \cdot 1} & \alpha^{2 \cdot 2} & \cdots & \alpha^{2 \cdot (n-1)} \\ \alpha^{3 \cdot 0} & \alpha^{3 \cdot 1} & \alpha^{3 \cdot 2} & \cdots & \alpha^{3 \cdot (n-1)} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \alpha^{(d-1) \cdot 0} & \alpha^{(d-1) \cdot 1} & \alpha^{(d-1) \cdot 2} & \cdots & \alpha^{(d-1) \cdot (n-1)} \end{bmatrix} \]

Formula 2. Reed-Solomon Parity Matrix

For those of you who followed me through all the other articles, we have already seen a matrix of this type, it is the Vandermonde matrix.

Perfect. Now that we have reached this point we can see two simple examples.

Ars Notoria 1

Suppose we want to build a Reed-Solomon code built over a Galois field \(\mathbb{F}_{7}\) choosing as primitive root \(\alpha = 3\) and \(d = 4\). A Reed-Solomon code defined this way allows us to detect up to \(d - 1 = 3\) errors and to correct up to \(1\) error. The reason is here. We can compute the generator polynomial \(g(X)\) using formula 1:

\[ \begin{aligned} g(X) &= \left( X - 3 \right) \cdot \left( X - 3^2 \right) \cdot \left( X - 3^3 \right) \\ &= X^3 + 3 \cdot X^2 + X + 6 \pmod{7} \end{aligned} \]

from which, knowing that \(G\) will have size \(k \times n\) with \(n = q - 1 = 6\) and \(k = n - d + 1 = 3\), we can build the generator matrix \(G\):

\[ G = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & 1 & 3 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 6 & 1 & 3 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 6 & 1 & 3 & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

and the parity-check matrix \(H\):

\[ H = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 & 3^2 & 3^3 & 3^4 & 3^5 \\ 1 & 3^2 & 3^4 & 3^6 & 3^8 & 3^{10} \\ 1 & 3^3 & 3^6 & 3^9 & 3^{12} & 3^{15} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 & 2 & 6 & 4 & 5 \\ 1 & 2 & 4 & 1 & 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 6 & 1 & 6 & 1 & 6 \end{bmatrix} \pmod{7} \]

Since \(k=3\) we can send messages of \(3\) symbols. Take for example

\[ \vec{m} = (1, \, 2, \, 3) \]

we have that

\[ \vec{c} = \vec{m} \cdot G = (6, \, 6, \, 2, \, 3, \, 4, \, 3) \]

and the parity check will be:

\[ \vec{s} = H \cdot \vec{c^T} = (0, \, 0, \, 0)^T \]

Let's do a super quick test. Suppose a corruption during transmission:

\[ \vec{c}_e = (6, \, 6, \, 2, \, 3, \, 4, \, \textcolor{red}{4}) \]

we have:

\[ \vec{s} = H \cdot \vec{c_e^T} = (5, \, 4, \, 6)^T \]

And we like it. Small note, this code allows us to have \(7^{6-4+1}=343\) codewords.

Ars Notoria 2

For this example we will not do tests and counter-tests, I will just build the generator matrix just to also use polynomials since finite fields allow us to (I will spare you again the link to field theory... nonsense, read here). Suppose we are in the field

\[ \mathbb{F}_{2^2} = \{ 0, \, 1, \, \omega, \, \omega + 1 \} \]

we have \(q = 4\) hence \(n = 3\) and \(k = 2\). We also choose as primitive root \(\alpha = \omega\), and \(d = 2\). We have:

\[ g(X) = X - \omega = X + \omega \]

from which the generator matrix will be:

\[ G = \begin{bmatrix} \omega & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & \omega & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

From this code we obtain \(4^{3-2+1} = 16\) codewords, and we can transmit messages of \(2\) symbols.
